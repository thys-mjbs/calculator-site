Results summary

This planner reduces the situation to one structural question: does reliable income cover essential expenses with enough margin to absorb normal variation. The key signal is the coverage ratio, defined as income divided by essentials. A ratio below 1.00 means the month is structurally short. A ratio slightly above 1.00 can still be fragile because most months contain timing mismatches and small shocks.

The output below describes the readiness classification, the size of the gap or buffer, and what that implies about the month’s stability. The numbers are not a moral judgement. They are a constraint map. If the ratio is low, the constraint is not “better budgeting.” The constraint is cash flow capacity relative to fixed obligations.

Use this summary to decide what type of move is required. Underprepared requires a gap closure strategy. Borderline requires a buffer build strategy. Stable requires a stability protection strategy. The rest of the report converts that into causality, constraints, and an action order.

Metric-by-metric interpretation

Coverage ratio: -. This is the headline. It tells you how many units of income exist for each unit of essential expense. Small changes around 1.00 matter more than they look because they determine whether the month fails or survives. The target ratio used here is - for a basic stability buffer.

Essentials total: -. This is the structural load that must be carried each month. If this number is wrong, the entire planner is wrong. If you excluded irregular essentials, the month can appear stable while still failing in practice. That is why the paid inputs include monthly equivalents for irregular essentials.

Buffer or gap: -. This is the immediate reality. A gap means the month is forced to borrow, skip, or deplete. A small buffer means the month is vulnerable to normal variation. A meaningful buffer means you can allocate surplus deliberately without breaking the base.

Causality analysis

Causality here means identifying which categories are driving the result, not listing every expense. Most budgets fail because attention is spent on small categories while the largest fixed drivers remain unchanged. This report highlights the top cost drivers and explains how much influence each one has over the ratio.

The “cause” of underprepared or borderline status is almost always a small set of rigid expenses: housing, debt minimums, and transport. Food can also be a driver when the household is supporting multiple people or buying convenience due to time constraints. Paid-only categories often reveal hidden structural load, especially childcare, insurance and medical, and irregular essentials averaged across months.

Your top drivers are listed as labels only. The goal is to decide where an intervention will change the outcome fastest. If the top driver is highly rigid, the likely solution moves from expense cutting to restructuring, renegotiation, or income expansion.

Constraint diagnosis

A constraint is the limiting factor that prevents stability. For underprepared households, the primary constraint is the deficit: essentials exceed income, so stability is mathematically impossible without changing either side. For borderline households, the constraint is buffer size: the month can work on paper but fails under normal volatility.

Constraint type also depends on which costs are actually adjustable. If your biggest driver is debt minimums, the constraint might be repayment structure, not spending behaviour. If your biggest driver is housing, the constraint might be the lease or bond commitment. If your biggest driver is irregular essentials, the constraint might be that you are not accounting for predictable once-off obligations as monthly load.

This section converts the diagnosis into a minimum correction threshold: how much income must rise or essentials must fall to reach the baseline threshold, then how much further to reach the stability threshold. Without those numbers, action planning turns into vague intention.

Action prioritisation

Prioritisation is not about what feels easiest. It is about what changes the ratio with the least time and risk. The fastest first move is usually a single structural change in a top driver, not ten small optimisations. The action order below is generated from the size of the gap or buffer and from the dominance of your top drivers.

If you are underprepared, the first priority is gap closure to 1.00. If you are borderline, the first priority is buffer build to the stability target. After that, a second move locks in stability so that progress is not lost to expense creep. The planner does not assume perfection. It assumes practical, repeatable moves.

The output includes a short list of action levers, ordered by expected impact. You can ignore a lever if it is genuinely impossible, but do not replace it with low-impact busywork. If a lever cannot move the numbers, it is not a lever.

Stability conditions

Stability is not a feeling. It is a set of conditions that, when met, make the month resistant to normal variation. The primary condition is maintaining a coverage ratio at or above the target threshold. A second condition is maintaining a minimum buffer, so that timing mismatches and small shocks do not break the month.

A common failure mode is hitting stability once, then losing it because essentials drift upward. That happens through small increases across multiple categories, not one obvious expense. Stability conditions therefore include a guardrail: a maximum allowed essentials total for the current income, and a minimum required income for the current essentials.

This section gives explicit thresholds that define “safe enough” for this baseline planner. If you have irregular income or high volatility, you should treat the stability threshold as a minimum, not a finish line.

Decision framing

Decision framing means choosing the correct class of decision for the result. Underprepared framing is about immediate survival and removing deficit. Borderline framing is about converting fragile months into repeatable months. Stable framing is about protecting the base and allocating surplus to a priority goal without destabilising the system.

This planner deliberately avoids lifestyle judgement and focuses on structural readiness. You are not being asked to “be disciplined.” You are being asked to make a decision that changes the constraint. That usually means one of three choices: reduce a dominant essential, restructure a dominant obligation, or increase reliable income.

If you make decisions that do not change the constraint, nothing changes. If you make one decision that meaningfully changes the constraint, the entire month changes. Use the thresholds and action order to decide what to do next with minimal ambiguity.
