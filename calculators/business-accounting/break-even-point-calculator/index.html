About this break-even point calculator

This Break-Even Point Calculator shows how many units you need to sell before your total revenue covers your total costs. You enter your total fixed costs for a period (for example per month or per year), your selling price per unit, and the variable cost per unit. The calculator then works out your contribution margin and calculates the break-even quantity and break-even sales value.

You can also enter an expected units sold figure to see an estimated profit or loss at that volume, plus a margin of safety. This helps you check whether your current sales targets are realistic relative to your cost structure, instead of guessing. It is a simple way for owners, freelancers, and managers to see if a product, service, or branch is financially viable.

Key assumptions used by this calculator

  • Single product or an average selling price and average variable cost across your mix.
  • Fixed costs stay constant over the period you select (month, quarter, or year).
  • Selling price and variable cost per unit do not change as volume changes.
  • All units produced can be sold at the same price (no stock build-up is considered).

What is the break-even point?

The break-even point is the level of sales at which your total revenue equals your total costs. At break-even you make zero profit and zero loss. Below that point you are losing money; above it you are profitable. In this calculator the break-even units are rounded up to the next whole unit, because you cannot sell a fraction of a unit in real life.

How should I choose the period for fixed costs?

You can use any period as long as you stay consistent. If you enter monthly fixed costs, the break-even units result is the number of units you need to sell per month. If you enter annual fixed costs, the result is units per year. The same logic applies to the break-even sales value and the profit estimate when you enter expected units.

What are the limitations of this break-even calculator?

This tool is designed for quick, straight-line scenarios. It does not handle stepped fixed costs, volume discounts, multiple product mixes, or taxes. For more complex situations you would normally build a full financial model, but this calculator is sufficient for initial pricing decisions and basic feasibility checks.
